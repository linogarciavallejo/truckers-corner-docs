The business in one minute

Truckers Corner is the daily operating system for a dispatcher — the person who keeps trucks loaded and moving. The dispatcher works with several carriers (trucking companies) and their drivers, finds freight from brokers (who represent the shippers), books it, dispatches a driver, tracks the trip, and bills it.

Today this is done by hand: searching load boards, receiving rate confirmations by email in dozens of formats, printing and signing them, coordinating drivers by phone, and tracking by calling. Truckers Corner turns that scattered, manual day into one system — and makes money by charging the carrier a small commission on each load booked through the platform.

Dispatcher — the main user (also the platform owner)
Driver — drives the load; uses the mobile app
Broker — sends the load & the rate confirmation
DAT — external load board (a source of freight)

How workflows get started — three kinds of triggers

A workflow doesn't "just run" — something kicks it off. Across Truckers Corner there are exactly three kinds of triggers. Spotting which one starts each workflow is the key to understanding the operation.

👤 User-initiatedThe dispatcher decides to do something (post a truck, assign a driver, search for loads).
⚡ Event-drivenSomething arrives or completes (a broker emails a rate conf, a delivery finishes) and the system reacts.
⏰ Scheduled / backgroundThe clock fires it (overnight compliance checks, 24h/3h pre-pickup reminders, detention timers).

A typical day

6:30 AM

Start the day — review compliance

1 · Onboarding & Compliance

The dispatcher opens the dashboard. Overnight, the system already re-checked every carrier, driver, truck and trailer against document expirations. One flag stands out: a driver's CDL expires in 8 days — the system already emailed a reminder. Nothing is blocked yet, so the day can proceed.

Trigger⏰ Scheduled A nightly job evaluates expirations and alerts 10 days out; it auto-blocks an entity only once a required document actually lapses. The dispatcher just reviews the result.
7:00 AM

A truck frees up — post its availability

2 · Posting

One carrier just delivered overnight; its truck is now empty in Dallas. The dispatcher posts the truck's availability: date, current location, preferred lanes, equipment (Dry Van 53'), and a minimum rate per mile.

Trigger👤 User-initiated Capacity became available, so the dispatcher publishes it. This is the dispatcher actively starting the workflow.
7:15 AM

Find freight — matching against DAT

3 · Matching

The moment the truck is posted, the system searches DAT for loads that fit its lane, equipment and date, normalizes the results, and shows them ranked by rate, distance and constraints. The dispatcher scans the top options.

Trigger⚡ Event-driven Posting availability automatically kicks off the DAT search. 👤 The dispatcher can also start it manually by searching for a specific lane.
8:00 AM

A broker sends a rate confirmation — book it ★

4 · Booking & OCR

The dispatcher accepts a good load and tells the broker (Allen Lund). Minutes later the broker emails a Rate Confirmation PDF. The dispatcher uploads it; the system reads it automatically — broker, rate, every pickup and delivery, commodity, miles, detention terms — flags two low-confidence fields for a quick human check, and the dispatcher confirms and signs. This is the moment that used to take 15 minutes of manual typing.

Trigger⚡ Event-driven The broker's emailed rate confirmation is the spark; the dispatcher ingests it and the OCR workflow runs. The broker — not the dispatcher — sets this in motion.
8:20 AM

Turn the contract into an order & dispatch a driver

5 · Order & Dispatch

Once the rate confirmation is signed, the system creates an internal Order. The dispatcher assigns a driver, a truck and a trailer; dispatch instructions land on the driver's phone; the driver taps "Acknowledge." Only then is the order active.

Trigger⚡ Event-driven Signing the rate conf creates the Order automatically. 👤 The dispatcher then assigns the resources, and the driver's acknowledgment confirms it.
Through the day

Execution — pickup, transit, delivery

6 · Execution

Automatic reminders fire 24h and 3h before pickup. The driver checks in at the shipper, snaps a photo of the BOL, and starts rolling. The truck streams its location — the dispatcher watches it move on a map, and the broker gets visibility. At the destination the driver checks in, captures the signed POD, and logs a small damage note. If loading or unloading runs past 2 hours, a detention event is recorded automatically.

Trigger⏰ Scheduled the 24h/3h reminders. 👤 the driver's check-ins and document captures. ⚡ the detention timer fires on its own past the 2h threshold. Three trigger types in one workflow.
4:00 PM

Delivered — bill it & collect commission

7 · Billing

With the POD in hand, the system generates the invoice (rate confirmation and POD attached), sends it to the broker, and charges Truckers Corner's commission to the carrier's card. The order is closed.

Trigger⚡ Event-driven Completing the delivery (POD captured) triggers invoicing and the commission charge. No one has to remember to bill — it follows delivery.
5:00 PM

Wrap up — the Master Web view

Cross-cutting · Reporting

The dispatcher reviews the day: loads in transit, pending confirmations, commissions earned, and profitability per carrier. Based on which trucks deliver tonight, they line up tomorrow's availability — and the cycle begins again.

Trigger👤 User-initiated The dispatcher reviews and plans. Tomorrow's "truck frees up" will restart the loop at Posting.

The big idea: most of the dispatcher's day is not typing data — it's making decisions (which load, which driver) while the platform handles the busywork.
